## Automotive Compliance Implementations for Swickard Marketing Automation and AI Implementation

### Executive Summary

Automotive dealerships face an unprecedented and complex regulatory landscape requiring comprehensive compliance measures across federal and state levels. With the Fifth Circuit vacating the FTC CARS Rule on January 27, 2025, attention has shifted to existing enforcement under Section 5 of the FTC Act and state consumer protection laws, where 92% of CARS Rule provisions were already covered. The implementation of marketing automation and AI systems across Swickard's 40+ dealerships must address critical compliance concerns spanning advertising regulations, data privacy, cybersecurity, and consumer protection.

### Federal Advertising and Consumer Protection Compliance

### FTC Act Section 5 Enforcement

The Federal Trade Commission Act Section 5 remains the primary enforcement mechanism for automotive advertising compliance, prohibiting unfair or deceptive acts or practices in commerce. Despite the CARS Rule's vacation, the FTC continues aggressive enforcement through state attorney general partnerships, resulting in significant penalties:

Illinois dealership group: $20 million settlement - largest in history against a dealer group

Arizona dealership: $2.6 million judgment

Maryland dealerships: $3 million settlement plus consumer refunds

### Truth in Lending Act (TILA) and Regulation Z Compliance

TILA and Regulation Z require comprehensive disclosure of financing terms in automotive advertising:

All financing advertisements must clearly state the annual percentage rate (APR)

Down payment amounts, number of payments, and finance charges must be disclosed

Any conditional offers must clearly indicate eligibility requirements

"Clear and conspicuous" disclosure standards apply across all marketing channels

### Consumer Leasing Act and Regulation M

Regulation M governs lease advertising with specific requirements3:

Lease advertisements cannot use misleading terms like "annual percentage rate" for lease rates

Monthly payment advertising triggers additional disclosure requirements

Lease availability must be accurately represented to typical qualified consumers

Security deposits and amounts due at signing must be clearly disclosed

### State-Specific Compliance Requirements

### California Automotive Advertising Laws

California Vehicle Code and DMV regulations impose stringent requirements5:

Dealer identification: All advertisements must include dealership name and DMV license number

Vehicle identification: Advertisements must specify license number or VIN (last 6 digits) and model year, make, model

Complete disclosure: All costs except taxes, registration, tire fees, smog fees, and documentation fees must be included

48-hour rule: Advertisements must be withdrawn within 48 hours when vehicles are no longer available

Certified used vehicle restrictions: Vehicles cannot be advertised as "certified" without complete inspection and documentation

### Washington State Dealer Advertising Regulations

Washington Attorney General enforcement includes specific prohibitions6:

Cost pricing restrictions: Cannot use "cost," "wholesale," or "reference" pricing without substantiation

Industry terminology prohibitions: Cannot use automotive or lending industry jargon that misleads consumers

B&O tax restrictions: Cannot charge line items for Business & Occupation tax

"Free" advertising restrictions: Cannot advertise "free" items that require vehicle purchase

Maximum penalties: $7,500 per advertising violation with each printing counted separately

### Texas Motor Vehicle Dealer Regulations

Texas Department of Motor Vehicles advertising rules under Texas Occupations Code § 2301.351 prohibit7:

False, deceptive, unfair or misleading advertising relating to vehicle sales or leasing

Internet provider notification: Dealerships must inform internet providers of responsibility for DMV compliance

Board rule violations: Any violation of specific advertising rules or general deceptive practices

### Alaska, Hawaii, and Multi-State Operations

Alaska Motor Vehicle Dealer Practices (AS 45.25.400 - AS 45.25.990) and Hawaii motor vehicle licensing requirements create additional compliance obligations89:

Alaska: Specific advertising restrictions and dealer licensing requirements

Hawaii: Both dealerships and individual salespeople must be licensed

Multi-state consistency: Uniform compliance procedures across all 40 dealerships required

### Data Privacy and Consumer Protection

### California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) Compliance

CCPA requirements significantly impact automotive dealerships serving California residents1011:

Personal information disclosure: Dealerships must inform consumers about data collection, use, and sharing

"Do Not Sell" rights: Websites must include prominent "Do Not Sell My Personal Information" buttons

Data deletion rights: Consumers can request deletion of personal information

Non-discrimination: Cannot deny services or charge higher prices for privacy requests

Penalties: Up to $7,500 per intentional violation, $2,500 per unintentional violation

### General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) Impact

While primarily European, GDPR affects automotive companies with European customers or operations12:

Connected vehicle data: Data generated in vehicles belongs to the driver under GDPR

Express consent requirements: Controllers must obtain explicit consent for personal data processing

Substantial penalties: Fines up to €10 million or 2% of worldwide annual turnover

### Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) Requirements

FCRA compliance governs credit report usage in automotive financing1314:

Permissible purposes: Credit reports can only be obtained for legitimate credit-related purposes

Written authorization: Best practice requires written consumer consent for credit checks

Adverse action notices: Required when credit applications are denied or less favorable terms offered

Proper disposal: Credit reports must be securely disposed of when no longer needed

### Red Flags Rule and Identity Theft Prevention

### FTC Red Flags Rule Requirements

The Red Flags Rule mandates comprehensive Identity Theft Prevention Programs (ITPP) for automotive dealers1516:

Written program requirement: Must establish documented policies and procedures

Four-step process: Identify, detect, respond, and update procedures for identity theft indicators

Senior management approval: Programs must be approved by board of directors or senior management

Employee training: Regular training required for all staff handling customer information

Annual reporting: Effectiveness reports required to senior management

Penalties: Up to $11,000 per violation per day for FTC enforcement actions

### Common Red Flags in Automotive Sales

Identity theft indicators dealerships must monitor include16:

Mismatched or invalid Social Security numbers

Income claims inconsistent with job titles or credit history

Employment at dissolved or unverifiable companies

Reused contact information across unrelated applications

High application velocity from single devices or IP addresses

### Automotive Cybersecurity and Connected Vehicle Regulations

### ISO/SAE 21434 Cybersecurity Standards

ISO/SAE 21434 "Road vehicles - cybersecurity engineering" establishes comprehensive cybersecurity requirements1718:

Cybersecurity Management System (CSMS): Required for all automotive organizations

Risk assessment processes: Comprehensive threat analysis throughout vehicle lifecycle

Supply chain security: Cybersecurity requirements extending to all suppliers

Continuous monitoring: Ongoing security monitoring and incident response capabilities

### UNECE WP.29 Regulations

UN Regulation 155 mandates cybersecurity measures for connected vehicles19:

Type approval requirements: Vehicles must demonstrate cybersecurity compliance for market authorization

Security-by-design approach: Cybersecurity must be integrated throughout development process

Fleet monitoring: Capability to detect and respond to attacks across entire vehicle fleet

Annual reporting: Manufacturers must report cybersecurity status to approval authorities

### Connected Vehicle Rule (2025)

The U.S. Connected Vehicle Rule restricts technology from China and Russia2021:

Model Year 2027: Prohibits sales of connected vehicles using Chinese or Russian software

Model Year 2030: Prohibits imports of Vehicle Connectivity System hardware from restricted countries

Compliance reporting: Annual Declarations of Conformity required through CARS system

### Financial Services and Lending Compliance

### Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA) Requirements

GLBA privacy requirements apply to automotive dealers offering financing22:

Privacy notices: Must inform consumers about information collection and sharing practices

Opt-out rights: Consumers must be able to limit information sharing with non-affiliated third parties

Safeguards Rule: Reasonable security measures required for customer financial information

Model privacy forms: FTC provides standardized privacy notice templates

### Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) Compliance

ECOA requirements govern credit decisions and adverse action notifications23:

Non-discrimination: Credit decisions cannot be based on prohibited characteristics

Adverse action notices: Required for credit denials or less favorable terms

Record retention: Documentation of credit decisions must be maintained

Dealer liability: Dealerships responsible for adverse action notices, not just lenders

### Cybersecurity and Data Security Requirements

### NIST Cybersecurity Framework Application

NIST standards provide cybersecurity guidance for automotive operations2425:

Framework implementation: Five core functions - Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover

Risk assessment: Comprehensive evaluation of cybersecurity risks across all systems

Vendor management: Security requirements for third-party service providers

Incident response: Documented procedures for cybersecurity incidents

### Automotive Industry Action Group (AIAG) Guidelines

AIAG Cyber Security 3rd Party Information Security publication provides industry-specific guidance25:

ISO 27002 controls: Information security standards adapted for automotive supply chains

Trading partner security: Uniform security standards across automotive partners

Risk-appropriate measures: Security measures proportional to risk levels

### Vehicle-Specific Compliance Requirements

### Odometer Disclosure Requirements

Federal odometer disclosure requirements mandate accurate mileage reporting26:

2011 and newer vehicles: Odometer disclosure required for first 20 years of vehicle life

2010 and older vehicles: Continue under previous 10-year disclosure requirements

Transfer documentation: Required for every ownership transfer

Fraud prevention: Measures to prevent odometer tampering and rollback

### National Motor Vehicle Title Information System (NMVTIS)

NMVTIS reporting requirements for vehicle history and title information27:

Federal reporting mandate: States, insurance companies, and salvage yards must report to system

Title brand disclosure: Information about salvage, flood damage, and other title brands

Consumer protection: Vehicle history reports help prevent fraud and undisclosed damage

### Enforcement Trends and Penalties

### State Attorney General Enforcement

Recent enforcement actions demonstrate aggressive multi-state coordination2:

Pattern of violations: Focus on undisclosed mandatory fees and deceptive pricing

Class action exposure: Private lawsuits following government enforcement actions

Compliance agreements: Detailed operational requirements in settlement agreements

### FTC Section 5 Continued Enforcement

Despite CARS Rule vacation, FTC enforcement continues under existing authorities282:

90% overlap: Most CARS Rule provisions already covered by existing laws

State partnership: Coordinated enforcement with state attorneys general

Industry sweeps: Coordinated enforcement actions across multiple dealerships

### Implementation Recommendations for Swickard Automotive

### Immediate Compliance Priorities

Comprehensive Advertising Review: Audit all marketing materials across all channels for compliance with federal and state advertising requirements

Data Privacy Assessment: Implement CCPA compliance measures and privacy notice updates for all California operations

Red Flags Program Enhancement: Ensure robust Identity Theft Prevention Programs across all 40 dealerships

Cybersecurity Framework: Implement NIST cybersecurity framework with automotive-specific adaptations

Staff Training Programs: Comprehensive compliance training covering all regulatory areas

### Ongoing Compliance Management

Legal Counsel Consultation: Regular review of compliance programs with specialized automotive attorneys

Technology Vendor Agreements: Ensure all marketing automation and AI vendors meet compliance requirements

Multi-State Coordination: Unified compliance approach addressing requirements across Alaska, California, Washington, Texas, and Hawaii

Documentation and Audit Trails: Comprehensive record-keeping for all compliance activities

Incident Response Procedures: Documented procedures for regulatory inquiries and potential violations

The regulatory landscape for automotive dealerships continues to evolve rapidly, with enforcement agencies demonstrating increasing sophistication and coordination. Success requires treating compliance as a fundamental business requirement rather than a technical afterthought, with comprehensive programs addressing advertising, data privacy, cybersecurity, and consumer protection across all operational aspects of the Swickard Automotive marketing automation and AI implementation initiative.
